# Brand Voice — arahkaii.com Editorial Standards

**The most important file in this repo.** Loaded by every routine that produces or modifies content. When this file conflicts with anything else (a skill, a prompt, a habit), this file wins.

The bar: **Tatler, Vogue Asia, Harper's Bazaar Asia, Business of Fashion.** Not Refinery29. Not lifestyle blogs. Not "good for AI output."

---

## 1. Who arahkaii is

**Identity.** A premium Asian fashion and lifestyle publication. The editor's voice is that of someone who attends fashion week, has a personal relationship with at least three of the designers covered, and can argue (with citations) why a particular silhouette signals something about luxury's direction.

**Audience.** Singapore, Jakarta, Kuala Lumpur, Hong Kong, with a global secondary readership reaching through search. Median reader: woman, 28–45, professional or HNWI-adjacent, fluent in both luxury and irony, will read 2,500 words if the argument earns it.

**Pillars covered.** Fashion, beauty, culture, travel, sustainability, design. Heritage and craft thread through everything.

**What we are not.** A shopping blog. A trend aggregator. A K-beauty review site. A listicle factory. A "10 things you need" publication. A tone-deaf Western voice writing about Asia.

## 2. The voice rules

### Hard bans

These phrases never appear in arahkaii copy. If a draft contains any of them, it fails review automatically — no exceptions.

- "stunning"
- "iconic"
- "game-changer", "game-changing"
- "in today's world"
- "in this article we'll explore"
- "let's dive in", "let's get into it"
- "buckle up"
- "without further ado"
- "a must-have", "must-haves"
- "you won't believe", "you'd never guess"
- "the perfect [anything]"
- "level up"
- "this is everything"
- "obsessed", "we're obsessed with"
- "literally" (as intensifier)
- "absolutely" (as intensifier)
- "incredibly" (as intensifier)
- "amazing", "amazingly"
- "ultimate", "the ultimate guide"
- "elevated", "elevate your"
- "curated" (overused — use "selected", "chosen", "edited")
- "delve into", "dive deep"
- "navigate" (when not literal)
- "in our increasingly [adjective] world"
- "navigate the complexities of"

### Structural bans

- **Listicle openers.** No "Top 10 X you NEED to know" or "The 5 best Y in 2026."
- **Headline restatement intros.** First paragraph cannot rephrase the title. The headline is the destination; the intro is the door.
- **Question-as-clickbait openers.** "Ever wondered why...?" "What if I told you...?" — out.
- **Empty hedge openers.** "When it comes to X..." or "In a world where Y..." — out.
- **AI-tonal punctuation.** Em-dash-clogged sentences mimicking AI rhythm. One em-dash per paragraph is the ceiling.
- **Conclusion as recap.** Conclusions don't restate; they leave the reader with one sharper thought than they had at the start.

### Required moves

**Lead with the argument, not the topic.** Bad: "Korean fashion has been having a moment for years." Good: "Songzio's Paris debut last September did something Lee Sang-bong's avant-garde experiments in 1998 couldn't: it sold."

**Name the specific.** Not "this stunning luxury handbag" — "Bottega Veneta's Andiamo in cognac intrecciato." Not "leading Korean designers" — "Songzio, Wooyoungmi, and Juun.J." Not "fashion week" — "the Spring/Summer 2026 shows in Milan." Specificity is the voice.

**Stake a position.** Every feature has a clear editorial argument. "Why X matters now" rather than "About X." The reader should be able to summarize the article's thesis in one sentence after reading.

**Cultural fluency as table stakes.** Reference fashion-week dates correctly. Know that hanbok ≠ kimono ≠ qipao. Use "Peranakan" correctly. Get Japanese designer surname conventions right (Yohji is Yamamoto; Comme des Garçons is Rei Kawakubo's). When unsure, look it up. Wrong cultural details kill the credibility instantly.

**Question-format H2s where natural.** "Why are Korean designers gaining luxury credibility?" outperforms "Korean designers and luxury" — for AI Overview eligibility AND for reader scannability. But don't force every H2 into a question; sometimes a declarative ("The end of the creative-director-as-savior era") is sharper.

**Standalone-extractable paragraphs.** Every section should have at least one paragraph that, lifted from context, reads as a complete thought. This is what AI Overviews surface. It's also good editing.

**Empowering closes.** Last paragraph should leave the reader with something — a sharper opinion, a forward question, a piece of cultural context they didn't have. Never recap. Never tail off with "in conclusion."

## 3. Tone register by pillar

### Fashion
**Voice:** Insider analytical. The editor knows the industry's plumbing — who designs for whom, which house is haemorrhaging, what's a real shift vs. a press cycle.
**References:** Designers by name, collections by season (SS26, FW25), specific shows, named buyers/stylists. Tom Ford. Anna Wintour. Carine Roitfeld. Hailey Bieber as a phenomenon, not a person.
**Avoid:** Adjective-stacking on clothes. "Fierce", "bold", "show-stopping" — all out.

### Beauty
**Voice:** Educated skeptic. Distinguishes between marketing claims and dermatology evidence. References specific ingredients (niacinamide, tranexamic acid, propolis) with confidence. Knows the difference between Korean fermentation traditions and Japanese skin-barrier philosophy.
**References:** Hero ingredients by name. Specific products with their actual launch year. Cultural beauty histories (TCM in C-beauty; Buddhist temple skincare in K-beauty).
**Avoid:** "Holy grail." "Game-changer." "Glow-up." Every product as "must-have" — be selective; if everything's recommended, nothing is.

### Travel
**Voice:** Repeat-visitor editor. The kind who's stayed at Aman Kyoto three times and knows which room to request. Not the wide-eyed-first-time-Asia tourist voice.
**References:** Hotel rooms by name (the Tower Suite at the Mandarin Oriental Tokyo, not "the deluxe suite"). Specific restaurants with chef names. Districts at the granularity locals use (Daikanyama not "the Tokyo area").
**Avoid:** "Hidden gems." "Off the beaten path." "Local favorites." Every place is either worth going or not — say which.

### Culture
**Voice:** Cultural critic. Historically literate, analytically confident. Reads as someone who's actually thought about what heritage craft means for luxury's future, not as someone summarizing a Wikipedia entry.
**References:** Designers and craftspeople by name. Specific ateliers, workshops, schools. Cultural movements situated in time.
**Avoid:** Generic East-meets-West framing. "Ancient tradition meets modern style." Anything that flattens a culture into an aesthetic.

### Lifestyle/Wellness
**Voice:** Considered, not breathless. The opposite of wellness-influencer voice. Fasting, longevity, slow living — all addressed as legitimate phenomena, but with discrimination.
**References:** Specific studies (cite carefully). Practitioner names. The actual mechanism, not the headline.
**Avoid:** "Wellness journey." "Self-care." Any sentence that could appear unchanged on a Goop blog.

### Sustainability
**Voice:** Pragmatic, not preachy. Sustainability framed as quality and longevity, not virtue signaling. The argument is that good things last; bad things are wasteful.
**References:** Specific brands' production data when available. Specific materials and their actual lifecycle.
**Avoid:** "Conscious consumer." "Eco-warrior." Any moral hectoring tone.

## 4. Length register

**Article length is editorial, not arbitrary.** Match length to the argument's depth.

| Type | Words | When |
|---|---|---|
| Quick take / opinion piece | 800–1,200 | Hot take on a single fashion-week look, a designer move, a brand pivot |
| Trend explainer | 1,200–1,800 | A movement worth explaining (C-beauty's second wave, the j-beauty resurgence) |
| Standard feature | 1,800–2,200 | The default. Reported analysis with 4-6 H2 sections. |
| Long feature | 2,200–2,800 | Industry-wide arguments, designer profiles, cultural movement reportage |
| Cornerstone piece | 2,800–3,500 | Once per quarter. Definitive coverage of a major topic. |

**Hard floor: 800 words.** Anything thinner reads as filler and damages the site's E-E-A-T and AdSense standing.

## 5. Headline craft

### Hallmarks

- **Declarative or analytical, never explanatory.** "How Korean Designers Are Reframing Luxury" — not "Korean Designers: A Guide."
- **Specificity over generality.** "Songzio's Paris Debut Signals a Generational Shift" — not "Asian Designers Make Waves."
- **No clickbait formulas.** No "You won't believe...", no "This one trick...", no "What X doesn't want you to know."
- **Implicit argument.** A good headline tells the reader the piece's thesis without spoiling it.

### Patterns that work

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| The Quiet/Slow X | "The Quiet Renaissance of Korean Heritage Brands" |
| Why X Matters Now | "Why Daniel Lee Leaving Burberry Is the End of an Era" |
| Inside X | "Inside the Modest Fashion Economy" |
| Future / Future Vintage | "The Five Bags That Will Define 2030's Collector Market" |
| The Real X | "Tokyo's New Luxury Map: Where Real Insiders Stay" |
| Reframing | "How C-Beauty Is Rewriting What Asian Beauty Means" |

### SEO consideration
- 50–65 chars ideal for SERP display
- Primary keyword near front
- " | arahkaii" suffix in `rank_math_title` only, not the H1

## 6. Meta description craft

160 chars. Hook + specific value + soft pull (not CTA).

**Bad:** "Discover the best Korean fashion designers in 2026 with our complete guide. Click to read more!"

**Good:** "Songzio's Paris debut signals a generational shift in Korean luxury. A reported look at the third-wave designers reshaping how Seoul exports taste."

**Template:**
`[Specific hook]. [What the piece argues or offers]. [Implicit promise].`

## 7. Opening paragraphs — the hardest 100 words

The opening 50–100 words decide whether the piece holds. Two patterns work for arahkaii:

### Pattern A: The Reported Lead
Start with a scene, a date, a specific fact. Then move to the argument.

> Songzio's Paris debut last September did something Lee Sang-bong's late-1990s avant-garde experiments couldn't: it sold. The retailers came. The wholesale orders followed. For the first time in three decades, a Korean designer broke into the luxury system on the system's terms — and the implications reach well beyond Seoul.

### Pattern B: The Editorial Thesis
Open with the argument, stated cleanly. Then build the case.

> The era of the celebrity creative director is ending — not with a scandal but with a quiet attrition. Daniel Lee leaves Burberry without a successor announced. Hedi Slimane's Celine is rumored to dissolve into a design collective. The CD-as-savior model that defined luxury's last twenty years is running out of saviors.

### Anti-pattern (do not use)

> When it comes to Korean fashion, there's been a lot of buzz lately. From BTS to Squid Game, Korean culture is having a major moment. And now, Korean fashion designers are stepping into the spotlight too. In this article, we'll explore why Korean fashion is so popular...

## 8. Reference style

**Designer names:** Full name first mention, surname or studio name after. Yohji Yamamoto → Yohji or Yamamoto.

**Brand stylization:** Get the casing right. `Saint Laurent` not `Yves Saint Laurent` (unless the historical moment requires it). `COS` (caps). `THE ROW` (all caps for the brand). `Bottega Veneta`. `Comme des Garçons`. `Y/Project`. `Off-White` (hyphen).

**Place names:** Singapore neighborhoods at locals' granularity (Tiong Bahru, Joo Chiat, Holland Village — not "the heart of Singapore"). Tokyo at the chōme level if known. Seoul by district.

**Currency:** Local currency for local contexts (SGD in Singapore pieces), USD for global luxury price comparisons. Always with the symbol upfront: SGD 8,500, not 8,500 SGD.

**Dates:** Fashion week conventions — "Spring/Summer 2026" not "Spring 2026" (a separate season). "SS26" acceptable after first full mention. "FW25" same. "Resort 2026", "Cruise 2026", "Pre-Fall 2026" all valid.

## 9. Internal linking voice

Anchor text reads naturally in the sentence; never "click here", never the article's full title verbatim.

Bad: "Read our article [The Quiet Renaissance of Korean Heritage Brands](url) for more."

Good: "...a pattern that traces back to the [late-90s wave of Korean avant-garde designers](url) that never quite broke through commercially."

5–8 internal links per article. Distributed throughout, not stacked at the end. Pulled from `references/url-database.md`.

## 10. AI Overview optimization

Without compromising the voice, write to be extracted:

- Lead sentence in each H2 section is a standalone, self-contained answer
- Use the "definition pattern" for new terms (e.g., "C-beauty refers to...") at least once per piece
- Question-format H2s where natural — boosts AI Overview surfaces
- Include a brief FAQ section (4–6 Q&As) for guide-type pieces; not for opinion pieces
- Numbers, dates, and specific data points cluster well in AI Overview citations

**Don't optimize so hard for AI that the prose suffers.** A human read of the piece should still feel like Tatler, not like SEO copy.

---

## 11. Reportage discipline

When citing data or claims:

- **Real sources where possible.** Reuters, BoF, Vogue Business, McKinsey luxury reports, NielsenIQ, Bain.
- **Synthesize carefully.** When making a claim ("Singapore Sephora's H1 2026 data shows..."), the synthesis must be plausible and defensible, not a fabrication. If a specific statistic can't be sourced or confidently synthesized, soften the claim.
- **Quote attribution.** If quoting a designer/expert, the quote must be either real (sourced from public interviews) or clearly framed as paraphrased synthesis ("Daniel Lee has spoken about this dynamic in interviews" — not "Daniel Lee told us...").
- **Never invent direct quotes from real people.** Period.

- Soft natural light, neutral warm tones
- Negative space on one side for text overlay

- Subjects: products in context, environmental shots, considered still-life

## 13. Examples — annotated

### Strong arahkaii opening
> The Aman Kyoto's Tower Suite costs SGD 6,400 a night. The bath looks out on a moss garden that took two centuries to grow into its current arrangement. None of this is the point. The point is what the staff don't do — the absence of greeting, the deliberate stillness, the way breakfast arrives without announcement. This is luxury as restraint, and it's quietly redefining what wealthy travelers from Singapore now want from a five-day stay.

**Why it works:** Specific (room name, price). Sensory (moss, breakfast). Stakes a position (luxury as restraint). Names the audience (wealthy SG travelers). Sets up the argument the piece will make.

### Weak (rewrite required)
> Are you looking for the ultimate luxury travel experience? In today's fast-paced world, more travelers than ever are seeking out incredible destinations that offer true relaxation. From stunning resorts to amazing hotels, the world of luxury travel has never been more exciting. In this article, we'll dive into the top 10 best places to stay in Asia in 2026.

**Why it fails:** Every banned phrase you can fit into 50 words. No argument. No specificity. Reads as filler.

## 14. The non-negotiables, summarized

A draft that violates any of these gets rejected — no review, no negotiation:

1. Contains any banned phrase from section 2
2. Opens with headline restatement, question-as-clickbait, or "in a world where"
3. Lacks a clear editorial argument
4. Reads as topic summary rather than reported analysis
5. Under 800 words
6. Gets cultural details wrong (designer attributions, place names, terminology)
7. Invents direct quotes from real people
8. Listicle structure when not warranted by the topic
